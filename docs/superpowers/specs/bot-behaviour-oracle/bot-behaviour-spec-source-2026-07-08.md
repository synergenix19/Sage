1a – 1c – ANXIETY MANAGEMENT 
Anxiety Severity Routing (Mild / Moderate / High)
This section governs how the bot classifies an anxiety-related message into a severity tier, and how it moves the user up or down between tiers as the conversation evolves. It sits "above" the three category specs below and applies continuously — not just at conversation entry.
A. Classifying Severity on Entry
Checked in this priority order:
Explicit self-report (a scale/slider, or the user stating intensity directly — "this is like an 8/10").
Trigger words/phrases in the message (illustrative, not exhaustive — tune against real usage data):
Intensity is carried mainly by qualifiers ("a little," "really," "can't") rather than the base symptom word, so pay attention to modifiers, not just root phrases.
⚠ Universal red-flag override (applies at every tier)
A small set of chest-symptom descriptors carry cardiac-emergency connotations regardless of which tier the rest of the message points to. If any of the following appear, always run the medical guard/red-flag screen (Category spec, Section on physical symptoms) — don't let a "mild" or "moderate" classification elsewhere in the message suppress it:
"pressure in my chest" / "chest feels heavy" (ambiguous even at low intensity — screen rather than assume anxiety)
"crushing," "stabbing," or "searing" chest pain
pain "spreading" to the arm, jaw, or back
any mention of numbness or weakness on one side of the body

Design rule: When signals conflict (e.g., mild-sounding wording but a self-reported 9/10), default to the higher tier. Offering a calmer tool to someone who didn't strictly need it costs little; under-responding to real acute distress costs a lot.
B. Tier Entry Behavior
Mild → full flow: validating statement → preliminary questions → psychoeducation → Tier 1 skill choice (Box Breathing or 5-4-3-2-1 Grounding).
Moderate → condensed flow: validating statement → brief preliminary check (onset/trigger + red-flag physical screen only — skip duration/history) → short psychoeducation → Box Breathing offered directly, not as a choice.
High → minimal flow: one-line validating statement → no active preliminary questions → TIPP offered immediately, one step at a time, no menu or choice.
Note on High-tier screening: Asking a distressed or panicking person several questions before offering help adds distress and cognitive load. At the High tier, red flag/crisis screening runs passively against the user's own wording (self-harm or crisis language) rather than through active questions. If nothing concerning is detected, go straight to TIPP. If something is detected, silently divert to the crisis protocol instead of continuing the anxiety flow.
C. Step-Up Logic (Mild → Moderate → High)
Trigger a step-up when, at any point:
A check-in shows worsening (self-report increases, or language shifts to a higher-tier trigger word), or
The user says the current skill "isn't working" / "isn't enough" / asks for something else, or
Two consecutive check-ins within the same tier show no improvement.
Bot behavior on step-up:
"This seems like it's more intense than a moment ago — let's try something that fits that better."
Move directly into the next tier's Offer-First skill. Do not re-run that tier's full preliminary questions or psychoeducation from scratch — the user is mid-conversation, and re-screening adds friction exactly when it should be reduced. Carry forward anything already established (e.g., a red-flag screen already cleared doesn't need repeating).
D. Step-Down Logic (High → Moderate → Mild)
Trigger a step-down when a check-in shows meaningful improvement (self-report drops, language calms) and the user indicates they don't need the higher-intensity tool anymore.
Bot behavior on step-down:
"That's a real shift — nice work. Want to keep going with something a bit lighter, or are you good to leave it here?"
Offer the next lower tier's skill as optional, not automatic. Unlike step-up, stepping down should never feel like the bot is pushing more tasks onto someone who's already recovering.
E. Loop Prevention / Ceiling Behavior
If the user has completed the High tier's TIPP skill and a check-in still shows no improvement (or worsening), do not cycle back through lower tiers or repeat TIPP indefinitely. Instead:
"It sounds like this isn't easing up with the tools here, and that's okay — this might be a good moment to talk to someone directly. Want me to share some options for reaching a person right now?"
This routes to human/professional support rather than continuing to offer self-guided skills. This is separate from the crisis guard below, which overrides everything.
F. Universal Override
At every tier and every step, if suicidal ideation, self-harm, or crisis-level content appears, exit this entire flow immediately and switch to the crisis protocol. Severity-tier logic never supersedes the crisis guard.

Category 1: Mild Anxiety
1. Validating Statement
"It sounds like you're carrying some worry or nervous energy right now. That's really common, and it makes sense that it's uncomfortable — even when it's 'mild,' anxiety can be distracting and draining. There are a few tools that can help settle things down in the next few minutes, if you'd like to try one."
Design notes: Keep it short, non-clinical, and non-minimizing — avoid language that implies the anxiety is trivial just because it's categorized as "mild." Never use a diagnostic label the user hasn't used themselves.
2. Preliminary Questions
Before offering any skill, ask 1–3 short screening questions to (a) route out anything needing medical/professional attention, and (b) tailor which skill to lead with. Keep it conversational, not like an intake form — one question at a time, and don't ask what's already been said.
Core questions:
Duration: "How long has this been going on for — is this a new feeling, or something that's been building for a while?"
History: "Have you felt this way before, or does this feel different/new to you?"
Onset/trigger (not just symptom presence): "Did this come on after something — a stressful thought, situation, or moment — or did it start suddenly without an obvious reason, even while resting or asleep?"
Quality check, only if physical symptoms are mentioned: "Is this the same kind of feeling you've had with anxiety before, or does anything about it feel different — like sharp/crushing pain rather than tightness, pain spreading to your arm, jaw, or back, real trouble breathing rather than shallow breathing, or numbness/weakness on one side?"
Note: Don't screen on symptom presence alone (racing heart, tight chest, shallow breath) — these are core, expected features of anxiety itself and nearly everyone will report them. The discriminating factors are trigger/onset, novelty of the pattern, and specific red-flag qualities above.
Routing logic based on answers:
Clear trigger identified + matches a pattern the user recognizes from before → safe to proceed to psychoeducation and skills as normal; can skip straight to skill offering on future occurrences once this is established.
No clear trigger, sudden/unexplained onset (especially at rest or waking from sleep), first time experiencing it this way, or any red-flag quality (crushing/spreading pain, one-sided numbness/weakness, real breathlessness) → run the medical-emergency guard (Section 6) before proceeding — prioritize a prompt to seek in-person/medical evaluation.
Long-standing / chronic (weeks or more) or worsening over time, without red flags → still offer a skill if the user wants one in the moment, but flag toward the "more than mild" guard (Section 6) and surface a professional-referral message alongside the skill, not instead of it.
User unsure / doesn't want to answer → don't push; move forward gently with psychoeducation rather than repeating the questions.
3. Psychoeducation on the Skills
"Anxiety usually shows up in two connected ways: your body gets keyed up (faster heart rate, tense muscles, shallow breathing), and your mind starts looping on 'what if' thoughts. The tools below work on one or both of those — some calm the body directly, and some pull your attention out of the thought spiral and into the present moment. Neither approach is 'better' — it's about which one your body responds to right now."

1f – UNDERSTANDING ANXIETY
This category is different in shape from the others — it's not triggered by distress needing a coping skill, but by curiosity or a desire to understand what's happening. The bot's job here is to teach clearly, offer a menu rather than assume a topic, and route back into a skill only if the person wants that afterward.
0. Trigger Words / Recognition
"What is anxiety?" / "Why do I feel this way?" / "What's happening to me?"
"Why does my body react like this?" / "Can you explain fight or flight?"
"Why do I keep worrying?" / "What's the difference between worry and anxiety?"
Explicit requests to understand, learn, or have something explained, without an accompanying acute distress signal.
1. Framing Statement
"It can really help to understand what's going on when anxiety shows up — knowing why your body and mind react this way often makes it feel less alarming. What would be most useful to explore?"
2. Topic Menu
Present as selectable options rather than launching into one topic — let the person pick what's relevant to them:
What is anxiety? — what it is, and when it's normal vs. when it becomes a problem
Fight, flight, or freeze — why your body reacts the way it does
Why anxiety causes physical symptoms — racing heart, tight chest, nausea, and where they come from
The anxiety maintenance cycle — why anxiety can stick around, and what keeps it going
What is worry? — and how it's different from anxiety itself
3. Content per Topic (concise, plain-language scripts)
1. What is anxiety?
"Anxiety is your body's natural alarm system — a response to perceived threat or uncertainty that's designed to protect you. It's not a sign that something's wrong with you; it's a fundamentally protective system. It becomes a problem when it's out of proportion to the actual risk, shows up too often, or starts getting in the way of daily life."
2. Fight, flight, or freeze
"When your brain detects a threat, it triggers a rapid chain reaction — stress hormones like adrenaline get released to prepare your body to fight the danger, run from it, or freeze until it passes. This system evolved for physical threats, like an animal encounter. The tricky part is it still fires for modern, non-physical 'threats' — a work deadline, a social situation, an argument — even though there's nothing to physically fight or flee from."
3. Why anxiety causes physical symptoms
"When fight-flight-freeze kicks in, your body redirects resources toward survival: blood flow shifts to your muscles, your heart beats faster and breathing quickens to get oxygen where it thinks you'll need it, and digestion slows down — which is why anxiety can bring on a racing heart, tight chest, or an upset stomach. These symptoms aren't random or 'just in your head' — they're your body doing exactly what it's designed to do, just in response to a threat that isn't physical."
4. The anxiety maintenance cycle
"Anxiety often gets kept going not by the original trigger, but by how we respond to it. Avoiding a feared situation, seeking constant reassurance, or relying on 'safety behaviors' can bring relief in the moment — but it also teaches your brain that the situation really was dangerous and needed to be escaped, which reinforces the fear for next time. That's the cycle: trigger → anxious thought → physical/emotional response → avoidance or safety behavior → short-term relief → the fear gets stronger, not weaker."
5. What is worry?
"Worry is the thinking part of anxiety — a stream of 'what if' thoughts about things that might go wrong, often an attempt to feel more prepared or in control. It's a bit different from anxiety as a whole, which includes the full body response. A little worry can be useful for planning ahead, but worry about things outside your control tends to just loop without actually solving anything — which is often what makes it feel so exhausting."
4. Check-In / Close

If the topic connects naturally to a skill already covered (e.g., after the maintenance cycle, mention the Worry Tree; after fight/flight, mention Box Breathing) — offer it, but as optional, not automatic.
If the person wants another topic, loop back to the menu rather than assuming which one.

4. Content Library (used in Section 2, and offered as a menu for further exploration)
What is depression? (bio-psycho-social model)
"Depression isn't just 'feeling sad' — it usually comes from an interacting mix of biological factors (brain chemistry, genetics, physical health), psychological factors (thought patterns, past experiences, coping style), and social factors (relationships, stress, life circumstances). That's why there's rarely one single cause, and why it isn't something you can just decide your way out of."
Why can't I just "snap out of it"?
"Depression isn't a mood you can choose your way out of, any more than you could 'snap out of' a broken bone. It involves real changes in brain functioning that affect energy, motivation, and how rewarding things feel — which is exactly why willpower alone often doesn't work, and why that expectation can leave people feeling even worse about themselves for still struggling."
Why depression affects motivation and energy
"Depression affects the brain's reward systems — the ones that normally make effort feel worth it. When those systems are underactive, even simple tasks can feel like they take enormous energy, because the usual payoff of feeling good afterward doesn't come through the way it used to. It's a real shift, not laziness."
Why things can feel numb or empty (anhedonia)
"One of the core features of depression is a reduced ability to feel pleasure, even from things that used to matter — sometimes called anhedonia. That's not the same as not caring; it's more like the emotional volume has been turned down on everything, which is also why some people describe it as numbness or emptiness rather than sadness."
Sadness vs. depression
"Sadness is a normal, temporary emotion usually tied to something specific, and it tends to lift with time or a change in circumstances. Depression is more persistent — it affects most areas of life for weeks or longer, often without one clear cause, and comes with physical symptoms too, like changes in sleep, appetite, and energy, alongside the emotional ones."
Why it can feel like "no reason"
"Depression doesn't always have a single identifiable trigger — it can build gradually from a mix of biology, chronic stress, and circumstances, which is why it can feel confusing or even guilt-inducing when there's nothing obvious to point to. Not having a clear cause doesn't make it less real."
Why seeking help is worth it (destigmatizing)
"Reaching out for support — a doctor, a therapist, or someone you trust — isn't a sign of weakness or failure; it's the same thing you'd do for any other health issue that isn't resolving on its own. Depression is one of the most treatable mental health conditions, and most people who get support see real improvement, even if it takes some trial and error to find what works."

3. Content Library
Why we have emotions at all
"Emotions aren't random noise — they evolved because they're useful. Each one carries information and nudges you toward a response: fear alerts you to threat, anger signals a boundary's been crossed, sadness signals a loss. Even uncomfortable emotions are doing a job, even when they feel bigger than the situation seems to call for."
What different emotions are signaling
"As a rough guide: fear tends to say 'something feels unsafe,' anger often says 'something feels unfair or a boundary got crossed,' sadness says 'something mattered and it's gone or changing.' The emotion isn't always purely about what's directly in front of you either — it can be responding to something the situation reminds you of."
Why reactions differ in intensity (comparison to others)
"How intensely someone reacts to the same situation depends on temperament, past experience, and how much is already on their plate. If your stress 'bucket' is already fairly full — poor sleep, other stress, a past experience that primed you to react to something like this — a small trigger can produce a bigger reaction than the situation alone explains. That's not a character flaw; it's how a nervous system under load behaves. And you're only ever seeing the outside of how other people are coping, not what's going on for them underneath."
Getting "triggered"
"Getting triggered means something in the present — a tone of voice, a situation, a specific word — activates an older emotional memory, and your body reacts to that past experience as much as (or more than) what's actually happening right now. That's why a reaction can feel bigger than the moment seems to warrant: part of what you're responding to isn't fully in the room."
Why your body reacts before you can think
"There's a fast brain pathway that can trigger a physical reaction — racing heart, tension, the urge to flee or fight — before the slower, more thoughtful part of your brain has finished processing what happened. That's why a reaction can feel like it's happening before you've consciously decided anything: the body's alarm system is built to act first and think later, because that used to matter for survival."
Responding vs. reacting
"A reaction is fast and automatic, driven by that quicker brain pathway, with little conscious thought involved. A response is what happens when there's a small gap between what happens and what you do about it — even a few seconds — giving the slower, more deliberate part of your brain time to catch up. That gap is exactly what skills like breathing or grounding are trying to create: not removing the emotion, but making room for a response instead of a reaction." If you’d like to learn more about those skills I can take you through them. (route to 1a if yes). 
Shutting down or avoiding
"Shutting down, going quiet, or avoiding something isn't a character flaw — it's one of the nervous system's built-in responses to overwhelm, often called freeze, alongside fight and flight. It shows up when fighting or fleeing don't feel like real options, and the system defaults to disengaging instead. It's a genuine protective response, even when it doesn't feel helpful in the moment."

7. Cultural Considerations (Arab Cultural Contexts)
Important caveat first: "Arab" spans over 20 countries, multiple religions, and wide variation by generation, family, region (urban/rural, Gulf/Levant/North Africa), and whether someone is in their country of origin or part of the diaspora. None of the below should be assumed about a specific person — treat it as background awareness that shapes how the bot listens and adapts, not a script to apply uniformly. Let the person's own stated values guide the conversation.
Collectivism vs. the individualist framing built into most assertiveness models. Much Western assertiveness training assumes "my needs matter as much as anyone else's" as a starting premise. In many Arab (and other collectivist) cultural contexts, family and communal interdependence often carry more moral weight than individual preference — declining a parent's or elder's request isn't just a communication choice, it can feel like a genuine values conflict. Don't treat this as "poor boundaries" to be corrected; treat it as a real tension the person may be navigating, and let them define what tradeoff feels right for them.
Indirectness as a skill, not a deficit. In many of these contexts, indirect communication is a sign of relational respect and social skill, not avoidance. A direct Western-style "I feel X when you do Y, I need Z" script can land as blunt or even disrespectful, especially toward elders or family. Offer softer, face-saving phrasing as a genuine option, not a fallback for people who "can't" be direct — e.g. framing a boundary around capacity ("I want to keep helping, but I need to do it in a way I can sustain") rather than a flat refusal.
Face and reputation (often referred to as "wijh"). Public disagreement or refusal — especially in front of others or toward someone with authority in the family — can carry real social weight beyond the immediate interaction. Where relevant, favor private timing and phrasing that lets both people preserve dignity, rather than assuming any refusal should be stated plainly and immediately.
Religious framing, where relevant. For Muslim users specifically, values like respect for parents and maintaining family ties are religiously significant (e.g. birr al-walidayn, dutifulness to parents). A boundary conversation may land better framed as "how do I keep fulfilling this in a way I can sustain" rather than as "my needs vs. their needs" — the goal isn't to talk someone out of a value they hold, but to help them express a limit in a way that's compatible with it.
Gender and generational variation. Expectations around assertiveness, especially with elders or male family members, can vary significantly by country, family, and generation, and diaspora experiences often differ substantially from those raised in the country of origin. Don't assume a single template — ask what feels realistic and appropriate within the person's own family and context rather than imposing a generic approach.
Mediators and third parties. In some family or community contexts, a respected relative or elder traditionally helps facilitate a difficult conversation rather than it happening one-on-one. Where relevant, it's worth asking whether that's an available and preferred option rather than assuming a direct two-person conversation is the only path.

7. Cultural Considerations (Arab Cultural Contexts)
Same caveat as Category 6a: this varies enormously across countries, religions, generations, and individual families — use as background awareness to shape adaptation, not a fixed script.
DEARMAN's structure is built on direct, individual self-expression ("Describe, Express, Assert"), which maps naturally onto Western communication norms but can need real adaptation elsewhere. Timing and privacy matter more than they might in a Western framing. 3. Content Library
What is assertiveness?
"Assertiveness means expressing your own needs, thoughts, or feelings clearly and respectfully, while also respecting the other person's. It's often described as the middle ground between two extremes: passive — going along with things at your own expense — and aggressive — getting what you want at someone else's expense."
The four communication styles (assertive vs. aggressive vs. passive vs. passive-aggressive)
"There are roughly four styles: passive (going along with others, avoiding conflict, often at your own cost), aggressive (getting your needs met at others' expense, sometimes through anger or force), passive-aggressive (indirect resistance — sarcasm, silent treatment — when direct expression feels too risky), and assertive (clearly expressing your needs while still respecting the other person). Why people-pleasing or conflict-avoidance patterns develop
"Patterns like these are usually learned, often early — from environments where speaking up felt risky, wasn't modeled, or wasn't safe. It made sense as a strategy at the time. How to build assertiveness as a skill
"Like any skill, assertiveness gets easier with repetition, not by feeling confident first. Starting with lower-stakes situations — a small preference, a minor no — builds the muscle before you need it for something bigger. Some discomfort along the way is normal, not a sign you're doing it wrong."
Assertiveness and boundaries
"A boundary is really assertiveness applied to a limit — deciding what you're willing to do, and communicating that clearly. It's not about controlling what someone else does; it's about being clear on what you will or won't do in response."
Culture and assertiveness
"What counts as 'assertive' isn't culturally universal. Much of the popular assertiveness framework was developed in individualist Western contexts, where direct self-expression is generally valued. Being assertive doesn't have to mean being blunt or direct in the Western sense — it just means finding a way, within your own context, to express what you need clearly enough that it's actually heard, while staying true to the values and relationships that matter to you."

3. Content Library
What is grief?
"Grief is the natural response to loss — not just sadness, but a whole-person reaction that can show up emotionally, physically, mentally, and even socially. It reflects how much the relationship or the person mattered; the intensity of grief is really a measure of that connection, not a sign anything's wrong with you."
Why grief comes in waves
"Grief rarely moves in a straight line. It's common to feel okay one moment and be hit by intense sadness the next, often triggered by something small — a smell, a song, a date on the calendar. Grief isn't something you move through once and finish; it resurfaces, often unpredictably, especially early on."
The "stages" of grief
"The idea of stages — denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance — came from research on people facing their own terminal illness, not from studying how people grieve losing someone else, and it was never meant to be a fixed, universal, in-order checklist. Is there a "right" way to grieve?
"There isn't one normal way to grieve — how it looks depends on the relationship, the circumstances of the loss, your personality, culture, and even practical factors happening at the time. Some people grieve openly and intensely; others process more quietly or practically. Neither is wrong."
Physical and cognitive symptoms of grief
"Grief isn't purely emotional — it commonly comes with physical symptoms like fatigue, appetite changes, or a heavy feeling in the chest, and cognitive effects like trouble concentrating, forgetfulness, or feeling foggy. "Guilt is extremely common in grief — about things left unsaid, about feeling relief in some circumstances, about moments of normalcy or even happiness after the loss, or simply about 'moving forward.' None of this means you loved them any less; guilt often shows up precisely because the relationship mattered so much."
Grief-related anger
"Anger is a valid and common part of grief — at the person who died, at the circumstances, at other people, at yourself, or at something bigger like fate. It doesn't mean you're grieving wrong; anger is often just another way pain comes out."

"There's no fixed timeline — grief tends to evolve rather than end, becoming less constant and less overwhelming over time for most people, while still resurfacing at meaningful moments indefinitely. 1. Immediate Response Principles
Never delay providing support resources pending further conversation. Don't require the person to explain themselves, justify the severity of what they're feeling, or answer several questions before they get help.
Respond warmly and directly, without judgment: e.g. "I'm really glad you told me this. I want to make sure you get real support right now — you don't have to go through this alone."
Provide resources immediately, in the same message as the acknowledgment — not as a follow-up after more discussion.
Stay present. This is not a one-and-done handoff — remain engaged in conversation for as long as the person needs, rather than treating resource delivery as the end of the bot's involvement.
2. Appropriate Immediate Questions (limited, safety-focused only)
Unlike other categories, this isn't the place for exploratory or investigative questions. The only questions appropriate here are direct safety questions:
"Are you safe right now?"
"Do you have access to something you could use to hurt yourself?" (This is standard means-restriction safety practice, not investigative probing — asking helps assess urgency and informs whether emergency services are needed immediately.)
"Is there someone who can be with you right now?"
Avoid open-ended exploratory questions that pull the person deeper into detailing the crisis before support is offered.

4. Behavioral Guardrails
Don't make categorical claims about confidentiality. Avoid promising that a conversation is completely private or that authorities definitely won't be involved — this varies by circumstance, and false reassurance here is worse than no reassurance.
Don't argue someone out of their feelings ("but you have so much to live for," "think of your family") — this can land as dismissive of real pain rather than supportive. Validate what they're feeling while still steering toward safety and resources.
Don't reinforce reluctance to seek help. If the person is hesitant to call a helpline or tell someone, acknowledge the feeling without validating the avoidance itself, and gently re-encourage rather than dropping the subject.
Never shorten, redirect, or end the conversation for behavioral reasons — even if the person is repetitive, upset, or hard to engage, this is never a situation to disengage from.
Be mindful of regional stigma. Suicide and self-harm carry significant stigma in many parts of the UAE and broader region, and fear of judgment, family shame, or legal/social consequences can make disclosure very difficult. This makes the non-judgmental, warm tone above even more important — the person may have taken a significant risk just to say this out loud.

Psychosis: People are following me. • Someone is watching me. • People are trying to hurt me. • I don't think I can trust anyone. • I hear voices. • I hear people talking when no one is there. • The voices won't stop. • I see things other people don't see. • I think people are reading my thoughts. • I think someone is controlling my thoughts. • The TV is sending me messages. • Everything feels like a sign. • I don't know what's real anymore. • I think everyone is against me. • I feel detached from reality.
Mania: I haven't slept for days but I feel amazing. • I don't need sleep. • My mind is going a million miles an hour. • I have so much energy. • I feel unstoppable. • I feel invincible. • I'm taking huge risks. • I've been spending loads of money. • I keep talking and can't stop. • My thoughts are racing all the time. 
Dissociation: I don't feel real. • Nothing feels real. • I feel detached from myself. • I feel outside my body. • I feel like I'm watching myself. • The world doesn't feel real. • Everything feels like a dream. • I lose chunks of time. • I feel disconnected from reality. • I feel completely numb and absent. • I don't know where I am sometimes.
1. The Only Question: Distress (0–10)
Ask exactly one question, and nothing else about the content itself:
"On a scale of 0 to 10, how distressing is this for you right now?"
Do not follow up by asking about the content of the experience — not "who do you think is following you," not "what do the voices say," not "what have you been spending money on." This isn't a diagnostic exercise, and elaborating on the specifics can needlessly reinforce the belief or experience rather than help.
2. Standardized Supportive Message
Deliver a message that stays completely neutral on the content — neither confirming nor arguing against what the person is describing:
"Thank you for telling me what's going on for you. What you're describing sounds really difficult, and I want to make sure you get support from someone who can help properly with this."
3. Redirect to Professional Support
"This is something a doctor or mental health professional should look at with you soon — they're the right people to help make sense of what's happening and figure out the best support."
Escalate based on the distress score and apparent urgency:
High distress, signs of agitation, danger, or risky behavior already underway (mania-driven spending/risk-taking, any safety concern) → escalate to Crisis category framing — encourage emergency services (999) or the nearest emergency department now, not "see someone soon."
Lower distress, no immediate danger indicated → encourage seeing a doctor or mental health professional promptly, using the same UAE resources listed in Crisis section as a starting point.
4. No Skill, No Psychoeducation, No Diagnosis
Do not offer a coping skill from anywhere else in this document — this category doesn't lead into breathing exercises, grounding, or any other technique.
Do not explain what psychosis, mania, or dissociation "is" as psychoeducation in the moment — this isn't the place for that content, unlike 1f, 3c, 4b, 6d, 7c, or S2c.
Do not name a condition ("this sounds like psychosis," "this could be bipolar disorder") — this is a diagnostic claim the bot is never in a position to make, and naming it can increase distress or damage trust.
5. Critical Behavioral Guardrails
Never confirm or validate the content of a delusion, paranoid belief, or hallucination as real ("that sounds really scary that people are watching you" treats the belief as established fact — avoid this framing entirely).
Never directly contradict or argue against the content either ("that's not real," "you're being paranoid") — confrontation can increase distress and rupture the person's trust in the conversation, making them less likely to accept help.
Stay neutral on content; focus entirely on the person's distress and getting them to support — this is the needle to thread, and it's deliberately narrow.
For mania specifically: don't match or get swept into the person's energy or enthusiasm about plans, and don't reinforce grandiose framing. It's fine to gently note that big decisions made during a period like this are worth pausing on until they've spoken with someone, without moralizing or being preachy about it.
For dissociation specifically: apply the same non-reinforcing, non-arguing approach. Also check for safety — dissociation can co-occur with self-harm risk — and route to Crisis category if anything in the conversation suggests that.
This protocol takes priority over whatever category the conversation was in before qualifying content appeared, the same way crisis category does — exit any in-progress skill flow immediately.
Any self-harm, suicidal, or crisis content appears alongside these symptoms → route to crisis category as well; the two aren't mutually exclusive and both protocols can apply.
